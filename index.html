Biography

Galactic archaeology, old stars, and open science.

I am an observational astronomer working in Galactic Archaeology. I study the infant Milky Way by finding and characterising metal-poor stars, especially in the Galactic halo where ancient stars and disrupted stellar streams preserve the Galaxy's early history.

My work spans catalogue creation, distance derivation, isochrone fitting, stellar streams, spectral data reduction and analysis, photometry and spectroscopy of metal-poor stars, and astrostatistics. I use Gaia, Pristine, public spectroscopic surveys, and follow-up observations from telescopes in La Palma.

I was born and raised in Chennai, India. I earned my BE in Electronics and Communication Engineering from Anna University, my MSc in Astrophysics from the University of Glasgow, and my PhD in Astronomy from the University of Groningen with Else Starkenburg and Amina Helmi.

I care about open, well-documented, reusable science and the idea of science for all. My codes and publication data are public wherever possible.

Research

Galactic archaeology with metal-poor stars, Gaia, stellar streams, and spectroscopic follow-up.

My first observing run in La Palma, during the Cumbre Vieja eruption.

In Galactic Archaeology, the Milky Way becomes a nearby laboratory for understanding galaxy formation. I work with ancient, metal-poor stars: living fossils that formed when the Universe had produced very few heavy elements.

Gaia gives precise positions, distances, and motions for nearly two billion stars. I combine Gaia with the Pristine survey, spectroscopic surveys, and follow-up observations to study stellar chemistry, kinematics, and the disrupted remnants of small galaxies that merged into the Milky Way.

These datasets help reconstruct the early Milky Way and connect our local, star-by-star view of the Galaxy with broader questions about how galaxies assemble across cosmic time.

Why is dark skin a joke?

A personal journey.

Credit: Homegrown webpage

Picture this: you are in a room full of chuckling folks, each delivering a witty one-liner after another. Laughter fills the air, and you are all having a grand time, unless you happen to be the punchline. Welcome to the world of jokes about dark skin in India, where the humour comes at the expense of people who happen to be a shade darker.

Sometime back, a friend attempted to convey a perplexing viewpoint: they believed it was abhorrent when white people made remarks about Indians and our brown skin, yet somehow acceptable when we, as Indians, cracked similar jokes about dark-skinned fellow Indians, mostly women. That paradox exposed a deeply ingrained mentality that it is acceptable to belittle our own people.

Beauty standards upheld by media and advertising drive these biases. The impact extends beyond jokes, from fairness creams to the absence of dark-skinned women in lead roles. Growing up, I experienced first-hand how casual remarks can carry the weight of exclusion.

Jokes targeting dark skin are not simply offensive because they imply a personal flaw. They highlight a systemic problem where dark skin is unfairly devalued. Comparing injustices does not diminish any of them. It reminds us to be allies for one another and recognise the different forms discrimination takes.
